## Technique Validation

### Temporal Narratives

**Validated Techniques**:
- ✅ Flashback: Used extensively in cinema since 1920s (*Citizen Kane*, *The Godfather Part II*)
- ✅ Flash-forward: Modern technique (*Breaking Bad*, *The Prestige*)
- ✅ Non-linear narrative: Established by *Pulp Fiction* (1994), *Memento* (2000)
- ✅ Parallel timeline: *Dunkirk* (2017), *Arrival* (2016)
- ✅ Bookend structure: Classic narrative device (*The Princess Bride*, *Titanic*)

**Industry Examples**:
- Christopher Nolan films (non-linear narratives)
- Quentin Tarantino films (fractured narratives)
- TV series: *Lost*, *Westworld*, *Dark*

**Sources**:
- Film editing theory (Walter Murch, Ralph Rosenblum)
- Narrative structure analysis (Syd Field, Robert McKee)

---

### Camera Movements

**Validated Techniques**:
- ✅ Static/Locked-off: Fundamental technique, Kubrick's symmetrical frames
- ✅ Pan: Basic camera movement since early cinema
- ✅ Tilt: Standard vertical movement
- ✅ Dolly/Track: Invented 1920s, perfected by Hitchcock
- ✅ Tracking: Following shots, Scorsese's *Goodfellas* Copacabana scene
- ✅ Crane: Epic movements, *Gone with the Wind* (1939)
- ✅ Handheld: Documentary style, Dogme 95 movement
- ✅ Steadicam: Invented 1975, *The Shining* hallway shots
- ✅ Drone: Modern technique (2010s+), aerial cinematography
- ✅ Zoom: 1960s-70s technique, Sergio Leone westerns
- ✅ Dutch Angle: German Expressionism (1920s), *The Third Man*
- ✅ Push In: Slow dolly + zoom, Spielberg technique

**Industry Standards**:
- ASC Technical Bulletins
- Camera operator guilds
- Cinematography textbooks

**Famous Examples**:
- Steadicam: *Rocky* (1976), *The Shining* (1980)
- Tracking: *Touch of Evil* opening (1958)
- Crane: *Gone with the Wind* (1939)
- Dolly Zoom: *Jaws* (1975), *Vertigo* (1958)

### Lighting Styles

**Validated Techniques**:
- ✅ Three-Point Lighting: Industry standard since 1930s Hollywood
- ✅ High-Key: Comedy and musicals, classic Hollywood
- ✅ Low-Key: Film noir (1940s-50s), dramatic cinema
- ✅ Natural Light: Terrence Malick, Emmanuel Lubezki
- ✅ Rembrandt: Named after painter (1600s), portrait standard
- ✅ Butterfly: Classic Hollywood glamour, George Hurrell
- ✅ Backlighting: Silhouettes, dramatic effect
- ✅ Side/Split: Dramatic portraits, duality
- ✅ Motivated: Realistic lighting, modern cinematography
- ✅ Colored/Gel: Stylistic choice, music videos, sci-fi
- ✅ Practical: Visible sources, realistic environments
- ✅ Hard vs Soft: Fundamental lighting quality

**Industry Standards**:
- ASC lighting guidelines
- Gaffer and grip handbooks
- Studio lighting setups

**Famous Cinematographers**:
- Roger Deakins (natural, motivated lighting)
- Emmanuel Lubezki (natural light master)
- Gordon Willis (low-key, "Prince of Darkness")
- Gregg Toland (*Citizen Kane*, deep focus)

**Sources**:
- "Set Lighting Technician's Handbook" by Harry Box
- "Painting with Light" by John Alton
- ASC Manual

### Creative Techniques

**Validated Techniques**:
- ✅ Bullet Time: *The Matrix* (1999), array camera technique
- ✅ Tilt-Shift: Photography technique adapted to video
- ✅ Long Exposure: Photography fundamental, light painting
- ✅ Hyperlapse: Modern technique (2010s), time-lapse + movement
- ✅ Cinemagraph: Invented by Kevin Burg and Jamie Beck (2011)
- ✅ Vertigo Effect: Hitchcock's *Vertigo* (1958), dolly zoom
- ✅ Rack Focus: Focus pulling, standard cinematography
- ✅ Whip Pan: Fast transition, modern editing
- ✅ Forced Perspective: *Lord of the Rings* hobbit effect
- ✅ Symmetrical Framing: Wes Anderson signature, Kubrick
- ✅ Glitch: Digital art, cyberpunk aesthetic
- ✅ Infrared: Alternative imaging, artistic photography
- ✅ Slow Motion: High-speed photography, Phantom cameras
- ✅ Monochrome: Classic cinema, artistic choice
- ✅ Split Screen: *Pillow Talk* (1959), *500 Days of Summer*

**Industry Examples**:
- Bullet Time: *The Matrix* trilogy, commercials
- Vertigo Effect: *Jaws*, *Goodfellas*, *La La Land*
- Symmetrical: Wes Anderson films, Stanley Kubrick
- Slow Motion: Sports, action films, Zack Snyder
- Split Screen: Brian De Palma, Edgar Wright

**Technical Validation**:
- High-speed cameras: Phantom, Vision Research
- Motion control: Milo, Bolt camera robots
- VFX techniques: ILM, Weta Digital

## Industry Standards Compliance

### Frame Rates
```yaml
cinema_standard: 24fps
tv_standard: 30fps (NTSC), 25fps (PAL)
slow_motion: 60fps, 120fps, 240fps, 1000fps+
time_lapse: 1fps, 0.5fps, etc.
```
**Source**: SMPTE (Society of Motion Picture and Television Engineers)

### Aspect Ratios
```yaml
standard: 16:9 (1.78:1)
cinematic: 2.35:1, 2.39:1 (anamorphic)
imax: 1.43:1
vertical: 9:16 (social media)
square: 1:1 (Instagram)
```
**Source**: DCI (Digital Cinema Initiatives), ASC

### Color Spaces
```yaml
rec_709: HD standard
rec_2020: 4K/HDR standard
dci_p3: Digital cinema
log: Flat profile for grading
```
**Source**: ITU, DCI specifications
